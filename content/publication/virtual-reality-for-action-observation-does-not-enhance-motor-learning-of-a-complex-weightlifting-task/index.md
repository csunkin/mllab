title: Virtual reality for action observation does not enhance motor learning of
  a complex weightlifting task
publication_types:

doi: https://doi.org/10.1123/jmld.2022-0069
abstract: Action observation, where a learner observes a model demonstrating a
  skill, has long been established in the literature as an effective means to
  acquire motor skills. Developments in virtual reality technology have made it
  possible for a 3D action observation viewing perspective, theoretically
  providing a viewing experience similar to observing a live performer. However,
  very little work has compared these two media and their effects on motor
  learning outcomes. In this current study, healthy novice participants to
  Olympic lifting (specifically the clean and jerk; n = 36) learned the exercise
  through observing a model demonstration in virtual reality (3D group) or on a
  computer screen (2D group). A third group (control) did not engage in action
  observation. Results indicate that following training, the 2D group more
  frequently used a three-step lifting pattern, and the 2D and 3D groups lifted
  with a significantly greater horizontal displacement compared with the control
  group. Also, the 2D group was more likely to use a proximal-to-distal joint
  coordination strategy during the second pull of the exercise. These results
  further the idea that only small parts of pertinent visual information are
  needed for action observation, and that virtual reality may provide too much
  information to novice learners.
